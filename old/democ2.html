Democracy 2 of 6- Rome: Capitalist Parliamentary Empire?

Published 01 Nov 2014

"Welcome Back! In this part 2 of 6, we will discuss the civilization of Rome and draw a comparison with the institutions of Capitalism and Parliamentary Rule as they exist today. Harold came back home. He was very eager to ask his father about Ancient Rome. He had googled it, but he could not understand a word that glared on the screen. He thought to himself, “why are these grown-ups so boring? Can’t they keep things simple?” His father told him about some emperor Marcus Aurelius’, and many lecherous, psychopathic and outright insane emperors who based their tyranny on the shoulders of one of the most advanced and disciplined armies of the world. They are marching throughout the world with their square shields, waving the banner of the golden eagle, that sent chills down every barbarian (Enemy of Rome) spine. An endless army of fanatics, devoted to chaos and murder. An army that brought home necklaces ripped from the necks of massacred women, rings from the chopped-off hands of dead enemies, and “protective” ornaments given to children. An army that served their people relentlessly, providing them with endless violence and instability. But Harold's father was wrong. His interpretation of Ancient Rome is too inaccurate and pessimistic to provide a proper understanding. Rome was not always a land bled dry by the likes of Nero and Caligula. It was once a village of wood and straw, ruled by a king. But that changed. Rome, since it's birth at the hands of Romulus and Remus was ruled by kings. We do not know much about the kings, because most of the data we have about them comes from myth and legend. And, of course, there were good kings and bad. But the last king was exceptionally bad. So bad, in fact, that Rome chose to gamble her existence on a new form of government rather than be controlled by him. His name was Tarquinus. Tarquinus the proud. He had usurped the throne, with no legitimacy over it. He had come to power with murder and conspiracy, and he ruled the same way. However, the people united, removed him from power, and was born the Roman Republic. Rome grew in power. After the vanquishing of the Etruscans, it was the master of all Italy. However, Rome was not going to stop at that. She (Rome) looked across the Mediterranean. She looked jealously at him. He stood tall and mighty. His sons ruled the seas. His sons taxed hers. His sons bullied hers. His name was Carthage. Carthage at its peak   We know the fate of Carthage. But we will talk about Carthage another time. The conquests of Rome are not more interesting than the government that controlled it.   feudalism.wikia.com The system which then ruled Rome was quite unique, according to Greek Historian Polybius: “The 3 kinds of government, Monarchy, Aristocracy and Democracy were united in Rome. And it was no easy task to determine with assurance whether the entire state was an aristocracy, a democracy, or a monarchy.” The beating heart of this system was the Senate, a “parliament” made of legislators chosen from elite families.  Its title was, as seen above, SPQR: Senatus Populusque Romanus (The Senate and People of Rome). These “legislators” chose two consuls to rule Rome. Only a member of the senate could become a consul. These consuls were the bosses of Rome, and there were two because:

  1. To balance each other’s power and ambition
  2. So that both tasks of state could be carried out properly, with one consul handling Rome domestically and one supervising her safety and conquest
These consuls were elected for an year. And a major factor deciding who becomes consul- Popularity with the Plebians (Non-Aristocrats). Thus we can see the unclear, silvery outlines of a parliamentary not unlike ours in Rome. Obviously more elitist though. These  senate played a role in almost every decision, from deciding whether to end the siege of Carthage to the construction of a new road between Pompeii and Capua. However, there was one fatal clause: the dictator. The dictator was chosen to rule Rome in case of a dire emergency, with power absolute. Many times a dictator was chosen, and almost always he lay down his power and retired. But, it was too good to last. With the term of Marius, the idea of one ruler re-surfaced in popularity. And soon enough, we had Caesar and Augustus. Now, let us see how Rome was capitalist. I came across the thought when I saw street vendors in documentaries on Ancient Rome. I researched And, it is Caesar to the rescue. We often see the politics of  First-World countries turning towards the advantage of the MNC’s (Multi-National Corporations), with the rich getting richer and the poor getting poorer. Of course there were no MNC’s in ancient Rome, but there was Marcus Licinius Crassus. Crassus was really “minted”. He was the richest man in Rome. He ran a fire-fighting company which doused fires AFTER receiving payment and an agreement to take ownership of the land which was currently on fire. Obviously, this made Crassus VERY rich. Caesar allied with Crassus and Pompey. Pompey was a very famous general. However, it was Crassus, who tossed the buck (Literally). Crassus was the man who vanquished Spartacus, and who was the very reason Julius Caesar, could become, um, Julius Caesar. The power that Crassus could hold while not being a politician or armyman is more of a reflection of our world today than 65 BC. It was not to last. The ancient world took the form in which we imagine it today. Crassus was made to drink boiling gold after a military defeat in the Middle East. Julius Caesar was stabbed to death in 44 BC. Augustus, his adopted son, won a civil war against his greatest general, Mark Antony, for control of the Roman Empire. Augustus brought Emperors, the senate fell in importance, the system was hereditary with far lesser need for men like Crassus. In 410, Rome was sacked by the Visigoths. The Roman Empire was no more.
