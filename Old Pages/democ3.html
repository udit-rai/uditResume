Democracy 3 of 6- The "Good Old Days" (Not Really)

Published 05 Dec 2014

"Spent bullet cartridges and dead bodies. The ancient towers echoed the desperate screams of the commander FIRE!!. The gates were being attacked by scores and scores of people, the guards had been overcome long before. The situation was so desperate that even an age-old maxim of war: He who controls and fights from the higher ground wins.. The hinges of the gate broke like a cube of ice struck by a hammer. The gate went down, unleashing a flood of angry men, women and children hell-bent on death and destruction, appearing like the army of Satan. They went into the fortress, struck down the few remaining guards. The commander had tried to talk for peace, but had failed. Marquis de Launay was executed. The prisoners were freed. The fortress is broken down, brick by brick.


A few days later, the monarch receives the news of this attack. He asks Is it a rebellion? The emissary answers No, my lord, it is a revolution.
The attack was on the Bastille on July 14, 1789. The monarch is King Louis XVI.
Most of us know the story of the French Revolution. The tennis court oath and the falling of the Bastille. It was a historic moment.
Let us sail 22 miles to the north.
Harold, a 22 year old peasant, is euphoric. He is finally old enough to vote. He goes to the voting bank, but is hauled away. He is distraught. He fights, tries to kick and punch everyone in sight, but in vain. When he complains to the authorities, the officers laugh. They taunt him and jeer at him. He walks away, humiliated.  William, his best friend, has seen everything. He takes Harold on a walk, and explains what just happened. Harold is too young, and does not hold any property. In the same booth, another man is hauled in the same manner as Harold. He had raised his voice at the fact that there was only one candidate.
This was England, in the Georgian Era.
Firstly, I bring England into this series because England was a constitutional monarchy by that time, which many consider almost the full way to a democracy.
The political situation of these two nations could not sound more different, but was truly very alike.
Corruption and decadence ran through the veins of the supposed democracies of these 2 sister nations.
Let us turn our attention to France.
The Declaration of The Rights Of Man and Citizen was established on August 1789. It states that: Men are born and created equal in rights. This was the constitution established in France. It did not even mention the king. Thus, France had become a democracy.
However, if a democracy like that of revolutionary France existed in a country, it would earn a lot of attention from The Hague. And, for claritys sake, we are talking about the Jacobin government. Jacobin France committed many acts we would call human rights violations today:
  1. Article 4: Slavery
  2. Article 5: Torture
  3. A section of Article 18: Freedom of religion

Let us look at the story of Saint-Domingue to understand further: Saint-Domingue, now Haiti, was under French control and one of their most profitable colonies in the Caribbean. This former pirate hub was then responsible for 40% of all sugar and 60% of all coffee. The entire economy functioned on slaves. And the conditions were too terrible to ever be accurately described in words. 40,000 slaves, more than the ENTIRE French population of the island, were imported every year. This was because conditions were so terrible that no natural reproduction took place. And, if somewhere a child was born, it was not uncommon for plantation owners to sell them off to other owners. In due course of time, the slave population represented 95% of the population.
The population was divided into a hierarchy of sorts:
  1. The Plantation Owners
  2. Free people of colour: Often born of white fathers and of black slave mothers, these were quite wealthy, however had to suffer from the racist laws.
  3. The middle class: These were the remaining whites, comprising of artists, workers and labourers, among others.
  4. The slaves: No words can describe their painful and demeaning status.
 
After the Declaration of the Rights of Man and The Citizen abolished slavery, huge protests broke out, mainly by the middle class because they thought it was oppressive to them. It ignited a fire since they already considered themselves the most suppressed since they did not own slaves. Wow. It is simply amazing to think that what we call democracy today managed to grow out of this. From this. Later, Napoleon re-introduced slavery, leading to the Haitian revolution.
Let us look at an English colony, India: India was called The Golden Bird, before its English conquest. Everything from Tax to Education was westernized and adapted for the benefit of the East India Company. The Company ran the country like a company, not as a government, which was very bad for India. Treasures like the Kohinoor diamond were lost forever. Indians were forbid from sitting in any compartment except 3rd class, it did not matter if the person was a barrister or a government employee. The police was formed, to do the dirty work for the Company. No Indian could rise above the rank of constable in either the army or the police. Perhaps this anecdote can explain the situation better than I ever can: During the rebellion of 1857, an Indian soldier fighting for the English was gruesomely injured during the fighting. He fell a little distance from a well. He simply lay there, helpless and bleeding out. Then, some English ladies passed right by the side of the well. There was a mug right in front of the well. The man asked for some water. One of the ladies replied: There is the water, my man, go and fetch it yourself. The soldier passed away not long after. These granted huge amounts of rights only to some, with the excess rights of the privileged robbed from the remaining.
