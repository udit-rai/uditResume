The Lord of The Pen

Published 23 Apr 2016

"It has been 400 years since he has departed. Yet his words live on; inspiring endless generations till today and perhaps endless to come. He has coined countless phrases and words, and given us spectacular dialogues like- All the worlds a stage, and all the men and women merely players. They have their exits and their entrances; and one man in his time plays many parts. To be or not to be, that is the question. If you prick us, do we not bleed? if you tickle us, do we not laugh? if you poison us, do we not die? and if you wrong us, shall we not revenge? Shall I compare thee to a summer's day? Thou art more lovely and more temperate. I believe you have guessed who I talk about; the bard, the legendary William Shakespeare. Little is known about his personal life; we do not even have a document of his birth. The documents provide data on the major events of his life; but they are merely proof of his existence, devoid of his enigma, the aura of his existence. Church Records tell of a William Shakespeare baptized on April 26 1564. We may not have the documents and records that tell of his personal story, but we certainly have more of such than we could have dreamed of in the form of his works. He has written around 38 plays, the Guinness Book of Records claims 410 on-screen movie adaptations of Shakespeares works, it is said he has invented 1700 words of the English language; and the Internet Movie Database claims he has writing credit on 1,140 films. Here is a list of some of his Plays by genre: I. Comedies-     1. A Midsummer Night's Dream A Midsummer Night's Dream, portrays the events surrounding the marriage of Theseus, the Duke of Athens, to Hippolyta. These include the adventures of four young Athenian lovers and a group of six amateur actors who are controlled and manipulated by the fairies who inhabit the forest in which most of the play is set. 2. Comedy of Errors The Comedy of Errors tells the story of two sets of identical twins that were accidentally separated at birth. Antipholus of Syracuse and his servant, Dromio of Syracuse, arrive in Ephesus, which turns out to be the home of their twin brothers, Antipholus of Ephesus and his servant, Dromio of Ephesus. 3. Merchant of Venice Though classified as a comedy the play is perhaps most remembered for its dramatic scenes, and is best known for Shylock and the famous ""Hath not a Jew eyes?"" speech and Portias speech on The Quality of Mercy. 4. As You Like It The play follows its heroine Rosalind as she flees persecution in her uncle's court, accompanied by her cousin Celia to find safety and, eventually, love, in the Forest of Arden. The play features one of Shakespeare's most famous and oft-quoted speeches, ""All the world's a stage"", and is the origin of the phrase ""too much of a good thing"". 5. Loves Labours Lost It follows the King of Navarre and his three companions as they attempt to forswear the company of women for three years of study and fasting, and their subsequent infatuation with the Princess of Aquitaine and her ladies. It features the longest single word noted till date: 'honorificabilitudinitatibus'. Histories-   1. King John King John, dramatizes the reign of John, King of England; son of Henry II and father of Henry III; the monarch known for his great excesses and careless manner, the monarch who was forced to sign the Magna Carta handing over much power to the nobles. 2. Pericles Whilst various arguments support that Shakespeare is the sole author of the play (notably DelVecchio and Hammond's Cambridge edition of the play), modern editors generally agree that Shakespeare is responsible for almost exactly half the play827 lines. It follows the life of Pericles; a prominent and influential Greek statesman and general of Athens during the Golden Age specifically the time between the Persian and Peloponnesian wars. 3. Henry VIII Henry VIII is a collaborative work, written by William Shakespeare and John Fletcher, based on the life of King Henry VIII of England; one of the most famous kings of England, remembered mainly for his six wives and decision to break away from the Catholic church merely because the Pope denied another marriage to him. 4. Richard II King Richard the Second is a history play based on the life of King Richard II of England (ruled 13771399); who succeeded the throne at the age of 10 and is later on claimed to be responsible for starting the war of the roses. 5. Richard III Richard III is historical play depicting the Machiavellian rise to power and subsequent short reign of King Richard III of England; the last king of the House of York and the last of the Plantagenet dynasty. His defeat at Bosworth Field, the last decisive battle of the Wars of the Roses, marked the end of the Middle Ages in England. It is worth noting that, though his historical plays are some of the fantastic pieces of literature ever written; they should be considered just that (fantastic pieces of literature) for the simple fact that they are inevitably and greatly biased towards the Tudors and against earlier rulers; as Shakespeare lived during the reign of Queen Elizabeth; a Tudor Monarch. Tragedies-   1. Julius Caesar The Tragedy of Julius Caesar is a tragic play. Although the title is Julius Caesar, Caesar is not the most visible character in its action, appearing alive in only three scenes. Marcus Brutus and Antony are seen many more times, and the central drama of the play focuses on Brutus and Antony. 2. King Lear King Lear, a tragic play, depicts the gradual descent into madness of the title character (King Lear), after he disposes of his kingdom giving bequests to two of his three daughters based on their flattery of him, bringing tragic consequences for all. It is said to be derived from the legend of Leir of Britain, a mythological pre-Roman Celtic king.

3. Hamlet Hamlet, a tragic play set in the Kingdom of Denmark, the play dramatizes the anguish of Prince Hamlet. Hamlets uncle, Claudius had murdered his own brother; Hamlets father, and seized the throne, also marrying his deceased brother's widow; Hamlets mother. He is called to seek revenge from his uncle, Claudius, by the ghost of Hamlet's father, King Hamlet. 4. Romeo and Juliet Romeo and Juliet is a tragedy written by William Shakespeare early in his career about two young lovers; Romeo and Juliet, who belong from violently feuding families. It was among Shakespeare's most popular plays during his lifetime and, along with Hamlet, is one of his most frequently performed plays. In the end, their deaths being together the two families. 5. Othello Othello is a tragic play that revolves around four central characters: Othello, a Moorish general in the Venetian army; his beloved wife, Desdemona; his loyal lieutenant, Cassio; and his trusted but ultimately unfaithful ensign, Iago. Given its varied and enduring themes of racism, love, jealousy, betrayal, revenge and repentance, it is considered one of Shakespeares darkest and most complicated plays. These are only 15 of his 38 plays, not to mention his 154 sonnets and five long poems. A long poem refers to a poem of book length. To say that the contribution of William Shakespeare to the English language is profound would be a gross understatement. He has redefined the English language to its core. At least in my minds eye, each one of his plays is truly a dish fit for the gods. He has shown us that Jealousy is the green eyed monster, that one can kill with kindness, that one can give the devil his due and say good riddance, that conscience does make cowards of us all, that you cannot have too much of a good thing. Today is April 23rd, considered to be his birthday, and records suggest is also his death anniversary. And so, I tell you with pride, Mr. William Shakespeare, that never shall you or your works vanish into thin air, that come the three corners of the world in arms to wish you a happy birthday, and that on the one fell swoop of the minute hand into the midnight hour all corners of the world have remembered you at once, and that you and your legacy shall live on, lasting forever and a day.
