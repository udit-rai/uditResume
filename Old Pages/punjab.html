Why Udta Punjab is required

Published 01 Jul 2016

Welcome Back! You all must have heard of the Udta Punjab controversy; firstly the struggle with the Censor board and then it being leaked two days before its release. The question I will attempt to answer here is not relating to the Censor board or its torrent leak; but why such a movie is required? Why Udta Punjab? You must have heard of Punjab, even those of you who live outside India (Especially those who live in Canada, US or Britain). It is the land of five rivers, of the Sufis and the gyanis, of saints and soldiers, of Pirs and Pathans, of fields of barley and barrels of beer. It is the land which has the honour of having one of the worlds earliest, if not the earliest universities (Takshashila); it is the land which hosted so many Sufis, saints and all of Sikhism. The sons of Punjab not only have proved themselves in education, philosophy, spirituality, trade but also war. The legend Bhagat Singh, who needs no introduction, was a Punjabi. The Khalsa Panth originated in Punjab, and still has its roots firmly fixed there. The Sikhs had re-written history with their bravery and valour; and also their propriety. Their tales of valour are known all over the world (Sava lakh se ek ladaun, to Govind Singh naam kahaun). But today that very land weeps tears of blood. The reason: Drugs. The abuse of Drugs has taken flight in Punjab, in absence of the flight of Industry and Employment. Many people, including some in the ranks of the ruling parties, deny it vehemently; accusing those who raise it as plotters against the good name of Punjab, while the opposition plays the issue to its advantage. But in this mud-slinging fest, the enervating truth remains; the number of drug addicts in Punjab are rising much faster than jobs, education and opportunities can save them. An article, detailing a venture by two Hindustan Times journalists, reports that An hour and 200 rupees is all you need to buy drugs in Punjab. They say that they ventured out at night, stepped in an auto, and the autowallah was helpful enough not only to take them to a multitude of spots, but also to but the stuff for them. It says the autowallah informed them that a packet of marijuana will cost Rs. 100 and one of Chitta (Cocaine) will cost them Rs 1000. A video by the two journalists has been uploaded on Youtube and Snapchat as well. They claim that the Police, instead of being prompt and active, dismisses the issue when they report it to them. ""It's as if we're sitting on a time bomb that can explode at any time,"" said Dr JPS Bhatia, who has run a rehab clinic in the region's main city, Amritsar, for more than 15 years. This is the state of Punjab today. But why? And How did Punjab take such a turnaround? The Question has many answers, but few solutions. The data on the issue is hotly disputed; a recent report by AIIMS says that there are 2.3 lakh opioid (heroin, opium etc.) dependent people and four times as many opioid users. The Survey also reveals that the majority of cases for Psychiatrists, counsellors etc. are drug related.

However, the Ministry of Social Justice and Empowerment dismisses the report. The minister, Mr. Vijay Sampla, has said Of the total 2.75 crore population in the state, the report estimated that over 8 lakh are drug users. The numbers include those who consume alcohol even once a week. The actual addicts are only over 2 lakh which is less than one per cent of the states population. A study by Guru Nanak Dev University in Amritsar suggested that as much as 70% of young Punjabi men were hooked on drugs or alcohol. There is a village called Maqboolpura, near Amritsar. It is called the village of widows. It is said that in every house in the village, there resides at least one widow who has lost her husband to drugs. A person interviewed by the BBC says One woman I meet has lost two husbands, and is now struggling to care for her new one, and her eldest son, who are both addicts. While the numbers are a hotly contested issue, particularly because Punjab is going to the polls shortly; one thing is clear: While reports and statistics can be dismissed and ignored, the issue not only has but continues to spread too fast to be dismissed or suppressed. As it has been said time and again, when you invite a wolf, you invite the pack. As per a 2009 study, nearly, 61,000 people are living with HIV and AIDS in Punjab as compared to 17,000 in 2007. As of December 1, 2010, 1,325 people have succumbed to HIV/AIDS. As expected, there is a nexus between the wolf and its pack. Experts believe a major contributor could be the callous and careless use of syringes by drug users.   There are many stories; one was shared by respected Supreme Court Advocate Mr. H S Phoolka of a 14 year old school-going child: He was caught by the police at the age of 14 when he revealed that he was taking drugs (heroin) since the last three years through his friends in school. It was shocking when he revealed that almost 28 boys in his school take drugs and are in this business. In rural Punjab, it is said that every well-off home has at least one addict. There are many theories as to why this issue has emerged. I was watching a video on this issue recently, an interview with Mr. Gurpreet Singh Toor, an IPS Officer, discussing various aspects of the issue. When asked about the cause, he replied: (Translated) Many people say it is due to the border. I want to say to those people, that Kashmir shares a border 1.5x larger than ours (Punjabs) and Rajasthan shares a border 4x larger than ours. But you dont have to ensure your kids dont smell of smack over there.  He said that the culture of Punjab has shifted from one that respects honest and straightforward workers, and penalizes them instead; something that has been exploited by the drug mafia. People nowadays dont want to earn 300 rupees toiling in the fields. They want to earn it in an instant, something the drugs industry facilitates. One time, I went to a wedding, and the family had spent 25 Lakhs on decoration alone. Imagine the situation of a straightforward, hard-working labourer. How can he sustain? Whether the issue is with Punjabs drinking, showing-off, machismo culture; due to the border; being the central hub of a supply nexus that stretches out from India to almost all of South and South-east Asia, the one thing that has no question mark next to it is the urgency of the issue. A solution is required, with an urgency that cannot be conveyed in words. An acknowledgement of this urgency would be a beginning.   Picture Credits: Cartoon- The Hindu Infographic- The Times Of India"
