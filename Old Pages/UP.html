A humble guide to Uttar Pradesh Politics

Published 10 Feb 2017

"Welcome back people!
Considering the most upheaving and roller-coaster times; I hope you will appreciate my indulgence in the very interesting event beginning tomorrow, the very next day -The Uttar Pradesh Legislative Elections; 2017.
Quite unfortunately though, the politics of Uttar Pradesh is highly, if not extremely polarized; and so discussions about elections here (along with many parts of India, if not all) inevitably end up with communal outlooks; victory lying with the one which has the most and the most influential communities in their support. Thus, todays blog may also end up revolving quite a bit about communities; along with the reputation, condition etc. of different parties. I have given a glossary of sorts below; so will be marking a name of a community with the entry given in the below glossary. So; without further ado, let me present to you a brief guide to Uttar Pradesh Politics/Election Cycle 2017- Apna Dal
The Apna Dal is a political party active primarily in Uttar Pradesh. It was founded by Sri. Sone Lal Patel on 4 November 1995. Currently; the party is a member of the NDA (National Democratic Alliance; the right-winged coalition currently in power at the centre). The party obtains the major chunk of its support from OBC communities (mainly Kurmis- (Kurmi/Kunbi_-)) of Eastern Uttar Pradesh. Its main vote bank is from anti-Yadav communities (like the Kurmis) who wish to counter the Yadav (Ahir/Yadavs-) domination in their area. The party is currently undergoing a huge feud between the Party President (and wife of founder Sonelal Patel) Smt. Krishna Patel and Mirzapur MP-and-Union Minister (and Krishna Patels daughter) Smt. Anupriya Patel. The feud is currently being fought in court; and due to this the party will not be able to compete according to Election Commission rules. Another Party- Apna Dal (Sonelal) or Apna Dal (S) has been registered with the Election Commission in lieu of the aforementioned by founder member Sri Jawahar Lal Patel. What is to be seen if the outfit can sort itself out in time or if the new outfit can draw the same following that the original did; or perhaps even outdo it? Rashtriya Lok Dal
The Rashtriya Lok Dal; or RLD in short; is a political party active primarily in Uttar Pradesh and influential in the western part of Uttar Pradesh. It was founded by Choudhary Ajit Singh; son of late former PM Sri Choudhary Charan Singh. The partys support comes mainly from the Jats (Jat/Jaat (Hindu)-) of Western Uttar Pradesh; who view him as their greatest leader after Sri Charan Singh. The party has suffered immensely from the Muzaffarnagar riots of 2013; which have pitted almost all of West UP into a Hindu VS Muslim competition; with caste and creed playing little difference. The party tenders mainly to the farmers; who now see neither their occupation nor their economic troubles; they only see survival through a Hinduvadi (Hindu-based) party. In districts like Amroha; the Muslims (Muslims) have an electorate majority of 30%; which places the Hindu community in great insecurity. The party now tries two ambitious moves on two political fronts: Bringing 20% Muslim candidates in West UP, to bring back the Jat-Muslim alliance that once brought the Late Choudhary Charan Singh to power; and recruiting defectors to play beyond its bastion; namely playing in East and Central UP. This election cycle may prove to be a do-or-die moment for the RLD; and the party is behaving this way as well. Congress
The Indian National Congress; also known as the INC or the Congress party; is the oldest political party of India; and one of the strongest. It is a major national party; with members of Congress holding the paramount post of Prime Minister 9 times out of a total of 14!  The party has launched its Chief Ministerial Candidate; Smt. Sheila Dixit. She is married into an affluent UP Brahmin (Brahmins-) family; an evident appeal to the high caste voters of UP. This decision has however; sidelined the lower and OBC communities of Uttar Pradesh; which can prove to be a catastrophic decision for the Party, which is anyways being considered as a sinking ship due to its losing streak since 2014. It is for this reason apparently, that the party has decided to tie up with the SP (Samajwadi Party); another key player and the current ruling party of Uttar Pradesh. Samajwadi Party
The Samajwadi Party; or SP in short, is one of the most dominant players of UP along with the BSP; along with being the current ruling party. The SP is the darling of the OBC communities; particularly the Yadavs and the Muslims (Hyperlinks for both have been given above). These two communities are the major votebanks of the SP; although the Muslims have also supported the SP in large numbers in the past. The Party is seen as extremely corrupt; and one not able to bring much results on the ground; though state sources along with many media sources differ from this viewpoint. The party is also not seen as one that promotes Womens rights; since Sh. Mulayam Singhs infamous statement on the Nirbhaya Gangrape Case- Boys will be boys, they make mistakes. The party has recently undergone a huge rift; similar to, if not larger than that going on in the Apna Dal (Mentioned Above). It is between the son, protg and current Chief Minister Shri. Akhilesh Yadav and on the other side stand his father and uncle; Shri. Mulayam Singh Yadav and Shri Shivpal Yadav respectively. Although the father and son have reached a compromise; Mr. Shivpal still stands out in protest, even speaking of forming a different party. They have allied with the Congress to stand against the BSP and BJP, and to draw upper caste votes. Bahujan Samaj Party-
The BSP; or Bahujan Samaj party is the other dominant player in Uttar Pradesh. The party is also viewed as not only immensely corrupt but also one that indulges in extremity, and at times unwarranted extravagance for personal promotion (through state funds). However, none can call the party shiftless; as it has undertaken great development projects in the NCR region; particularly Greater Noida during its reign.  The party caters to the lower classes; particularly the Dalits (Dalits). It is an extremely pro-Dalit party; with the party leader; Smt. Mayawati Kumari a Dalit herself. They can also be called a blue-eyed boy for the Muslims; as they have supported them in huge numbers in the past. They face a huge challenge in the form of the SP-Congress alliance on one hand; and the BJP on the other. What is to be seen is if, and how they maneuver through this tricky time to reclaim a throne that was once theirs. Bharatiya Janta Party-
Last, but certainly not the least, is the Bhartiya Janta Party. It is the ruling party in the centre; and is viewed as an extremely active and least corrupt party competing this time around. The Party has always been the apple of the eye of the Upper Caste voters of UP; particularly the Brahmins. The party is viewed as a Hindu-Vadi party; which brings it great open-ness among the Hindus of West UP; still reeling from the bloodshed and slaughter of 2013, and desperate to keep the reins of power in Hindu hands. The Muslim community is just as paranoid of the party; desperate to not let them come to power out of fear of violence and bias from Hindu hands. The Party has some support from the Gujjars (Gujjar/Gurjar), and the Lodhs (as Ex-CM of UP Sh. Kalyan Singh was a Lodh BJP CM) (Lodh/Lodhi-). Speaking of West UP, however, the party may suffer from losses due to the fact that the dominant Jat community of West UP is extremely angry with the party due to the reservation issue; for which the Jats are at protests in Haryana as you read this blog. The party is also a favourite with the economically middle classes after the demonetization issue; which proved its reputation as a party with its priorities towards administration and removal of corruption.

This is a glossary of a few of the important communities that dominate (dominate refers to domination of land, money and political organization) in different regions of Uttar Pradesh (Not in any particular order) along with a short note about them (professions, socio-economic conditions, beliefs, alliances or animosities with other communities etc that may have an impact in the elections)-
Ahir/Yadavs- One of the most influential communities in Uttar Pradesh; the Ahir community is an OBC community that dominates almost the entirety of Eastern UP and Bihar, parts of Western UP, and even parts of Haryana, Gujarat and a few parts of Punjab. The Yadav-vanshis or yaduvanshis as they also call themselves claim descent from Lord Krishna. They are almost entirely Hindu. They may be called a Kshatriya community due to their martial roots but had since taken to cattle-herding and hazing; which brought them down, in certain eyes, to shudra status. Thus, they are stuck between the hierarchy, with the traditional upper castes rejecting them as a lower caste and the lower castes rejecting them as an upper caste. This has caused great politicization and communal identity in these people; which, along with their numbers, makes them a game-changer if not puppeteer in the electoral politics of both Uttar Pradesh and Bihar.
Brahmins- The Brahmins (along with the Bhumihar community) form approximately 11% of the electorate in Uttar Pradesh. However, the community has an almost monopoly over the states media, bureaucracy and educational institutions. They are traditionally priests and teachers and are considered the highest caste in the caste system, and this sense of superiority plays a huge role in the Brahmin ideology. The community once had an absolute monopoly over Uttar Pradesh politics, and many Brahmins wish for that dominance to return in their hands. Their monopoly was so great that The Brahmins are a very powerful community, largely right-winged and have a kingmaker like presence in Uttar Pradesh.
Muslims The Muslims are not a community per se, however their role in Uttar Pradesh politics is akin to that of a community. They are the least literate; according to Asaduddin Owaisi, President of AIMIM, and in 2004-2005, half of the community fell below the poverty line. The community also suffers from other problems, such as Islamophobia, communal violence and terrorism. They form 19.3% of the population of Uttar Pradesh; and have their condition in Uttar Pradesh(along with some other areas) is akin to that of European Jews in the medieval era. They have become largely marginalized and isolated due to these factors; and thus the communal sentiment, polarization and the political organization of the community is extremely high; if not the highest. They form more than 20% of the electorate; and are the most reliable to vote en bloc. Dalits
The term Dalit means oppressed. Their very name gives away the millennia of unjustified, unimaginable oppression they have suffered under the caste system. There were traditionally 4 varnas in the caste system; and each community fell into one of them- Brahmins (Mentioned Above), Kshatriyas (Warriors and Rulers), Banias/Vaishyas (Tradesmen) and Shudras (Working class people). A huge group of people was completely left out of this system; the cobblers, leather workers, toilet cleaners etc. These were simply declared untouchable; the doctrine of purity dictates where they are and are not allowed to live, go, or sit; who they can and cannot give water to, eat with, or marry; extending into the minutia of all aspects of daily life. These laws are so extreme that not only were the Dalits not allowed into the villages; but even the killing of a Dalit for drawing water from the village well is not uncommon even today. The community is thus the most oppressed and backward; however reservation has uplifted their status to a limited extent. The community has an alliance of sorts with the Muslims (as a majority of Muslims are believed to be Dalits who converted when Islam arrived in India; so as to escape the shackles of the caste system). They also have a huge degree of political organization, along with a strong communal identity.  
Gujjar/Gurjar The Gujjars are an OBC land-owning community of Uttar Pradesh. They are traditionally a pastoral community; however nowadays many have settled farms and animal husbandry. A lot of the community (particularly in cities) is in the dairy business; along with a sizeable presence in the army and police forces. They are considered as a backward class; even granted Scheduled Tribe status in Jammu and Kashmir. The community lacks in education; particularly in female education. They are a financially sound community in Uttar Pradesh; with a majority having access to basic facilities and education. However, very few pursue higher education. The community is largely right-winged and is politically active (also tend to vote en bloc); with protests slipping into caste-based violence and rioting multiple times (in Rajasthan). The community is not biased towards any particular party; only to their community.
Lodh/Lodhi- The Lodhs are another OBC agricultural community of Uttar Pradesh. Traditionally involved in farming; nowadays many are involved in the lentils and vegetable industry, working in all professions involved; from shopkeepers to processors, to transporters to packers; among other professions. The community is a backward community which claims Rajput (One of the Highest castes, second only to the Brahmins according to the caste hierarchy) status; which places them very high on the caste hierarchy. The community is moderately educated (above average literacy rates but dismal higher education rates) and places high value on maintaining traditions. The community is well-represented in regional politics, and many pursue Govt, police or army careers as well. The community is extremely right-winged; particularly because the most important leader of UP Lodh Rajputs; Mr. Kalyan Singh, belongs to the BJP (A right winged party).  

 

Jat Reservation Protests


Jat/Jaat (Hindu)- The Jats are a traditionally agrarian community with a powerful presence (to say the least) in Western Uttar Pradesh. They have a huge presence over 19 districts of Western Uttar Pradesh. They also have a huge influence in Haryana, Punjab and Rajasthan along with lesser influence in many other states. They are a powerful land-owning community which is highly politically organized and tend to vote en bloc. The Jats of Uttar Pradesh (on an average) vehemently dislike the Muslims; and it is the same from the Muslim side as well. Many people of the community are engaged in the sugarcane business; mainly as farmers. The sugarcane industry of Uttar Pradesh is falling; and the subsequent financial situations of the mills has left many farmers in a dire state; even pushing many once wealthy farmers below the poverty line. This; coupled with todays fast-paced economy which is snatching capital from the Primary sector at ballistic speed, has led to a rampant sense of insecurity in the community. Also, when the Supreme Court removed Jats from the OBC list in 2015, there were widespread protests, and many legal battles which culminated in the Haryana Jat Reservation debacle in February of 2016; which was spreading in Rajasthan and Uttar Pradesh as well. The OBC issue is also a very important one for the community; along with their right-winged stance, and their newfound economic troubles: The Jat vote is expected to be a game-changer in Western Uttar Pradesh; if not the entire state.
Kurmi/Kunbi- The Kurmis are another OBC agrarian community of Uttar Pradesh; along with considerable presence in Bihar as well. They are spread over various other states as well. They are traditionally engaged in agriculture; and stand on a similar stand as the Yadavs on the caste hierarchy. This has caused friction between the two communities; which has resulted in animosity between the two in Uttar Pradesh; while the Janta Dal has managed to make an alliance of the two in Bihar. They are a relatively industrious community; with many members engaged in other professions and others serving in the army, police forces etc. Many are involved in the vegetables and lentils business. The Kurmis are highly opposed to the Yadav domination of the OBC population of Uttar Pradesh; if not Uttar Pradesh in general. This causes the Kurmis to be electoral opponents of the Yadavs. The Kurmis form about 9% of Uttar Pradeshs population, and 24% percent of the OBC population. However; some Kurmi leaders oppose these statistics, citing the Pro-Yadav government. As can be seen; there is a story to be found beneath every stone. Thus I have attempted to cover the major and most prominent stories, factors etc that are shaping the future of UP and this country as we speak. In the end; every party that comes to power will favour its votebank, and thus every votebank has to make its presence felt in order for truly fair elections to be held. What I mean to say is that everyone must vote; only when every section of society makes its presence felt; will every section of society receive the attention and care that they both need and deserve.
